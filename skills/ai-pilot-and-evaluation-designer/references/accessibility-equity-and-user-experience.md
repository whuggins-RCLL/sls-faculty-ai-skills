# Accessibility, equity, and user experience

A workflow that performs well on average can still exclude or disadvantage some users. Build accessibility and equity into the pilot, not into a later afterthought.

## Accessibility to examine

- Keyboard-only use
- Screen-reader access
- Captioning of any audio or video
- Accessibility of documents the system produces (headings, alt text, reading order)
- Cognitive burden and clarity
- Error-message clarity
- Language access
- Account access requirements
- Dependence on a paid tier
- Training burden to use the tool
- Availability of an alternative workflow for those who cannot or choose not to use it

## Equity to examine

- Do outcomes differ across groups of users or students?
- Does the tool assume resources (devices, subscriptions, connectivity) not everyone has?
- Is there a fair, non-penalizing alternative?
- Can a user contest or opt out of an AI-influenced outcome?

## Method

- Include accessibility cases in the test set.
- Where relevant, include reviewers or testers who use assistive technology.
- Record accessibility failures as their own category; a significant accessibility exclusion can be a critical failure.

## Do not

- Do not claim formal accessibility compliance (for example, a specific standard) without qualified review.
- Do not treat accessibility as satisfied because the interface "looks usable."
