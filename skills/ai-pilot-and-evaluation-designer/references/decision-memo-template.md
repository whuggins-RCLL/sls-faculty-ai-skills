# Decision memo template

The decision memo turns pilot evidence into a recommendation an authorized decision-maker can act on. It is honest about limits and uses only calibrated labels.

## Structure

### 1. Decision and options
The decision this pilot supports and the realistic options.

### 2. Bottom line
One calibrated label (see below) and one or two sentences of why.

### 3. What was tested
System identity and configuration, test and research-checked dates, test-set summary with denominators, baseline, and review method.

### 4. Results against predetermined criteria
How outcomes compared with the thresholds set before testing. Report dimensions separately.

### 5. Critical failures
Any critical failures, stated plainly; note that these are not offset by strong average performance.

### 6. Omissions and review burden
Omission findings and the realistic human-review cost, including the verification-cost check.

### 7. Data, permissions, accessibility, equity
Findings and any open institutional questions.

### 8. Limitations
What the sample and method can and cannot establish.

### 9. Recommendation and next step
The labeled recommendation and the concrete next action, including any required authorized review.

## Calibrated labels (use only these)

Not pilot-ready; Ready for a narrow pilot; Ready for a structured pilot; Evidence supports stopping; Evidence supports revision and retesting; Evidence supports continued limited exploration; Evidence supports a larger evaluation; Institutional review required; Evidence insufficient for a decision; Deployment planning may be considered by authorized decision-makers.

Never: Approved, Certified, Safe, Compliant, Proven, Ready for Stanford-wide deployment.

## Negative results

A "stop" or "insufficient evidence" outcome is a successful pilot result. Present it as useful evidence that prevented a costly mistake, not as a failure of the pilot.
