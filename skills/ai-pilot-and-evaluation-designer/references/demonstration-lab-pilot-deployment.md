# Demonstration, lab, pilot, deployment

Faculty often collapse four different things into one word: "we tried it." Keep them distinct, because each supports a different conclusion.

## The four states

| State | Question it answers | What it can establish | What it cannot establish |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demonstration | Can it produce this result once? | Existence of a capability under chosen conditions | Reliability, representativeness, workflow fit |
| Capability lab | What happened under documented conditions? | A behavior occurred, with evidence, on defined inputs | That the full workflow performs acceptably |
| Pilot | Does the complete workflow perform acceptably under representative, limited, documented conditions? | Workflow performance, review burden, failure profile, suitability signals | Long-term operational reliability at full scale |
| Deployment | Does it work in continuing operational use? | Operational performance and maintenance reality | (This Skill does not authorize or perform deployment) |

## Common confusions to correct

- A polished vendor demo selects the inputs and the framing. It is a demonstration, not evidence of representative performance.
- One successful five-minute experiment is a capability observation, not a pilot.
- "It worked for me a few times" is convenience sampling, not a workflow result.
- A good pilot result is evidence for a decision, not institutional approval to deploy.

## How to move up a level

- Demonstration → capability lab: fix the inputs, document conditions, define what counts as success and failure. (Route single behaviors to the Capability and Failure Lab.)
- Capability lab → pilot: add a baseline, a representative and difficult test set, human review measurement, data and permission limits, and a decision.
- Pilot → deployment planning: only an authorized decision-maker, after evidence and institutional review, may consider it.

## Handoff

If the faculty member actually wants to test one behavior directly, route to the Capability and Failure Lab and return when a workflow-level decision is at stake.
