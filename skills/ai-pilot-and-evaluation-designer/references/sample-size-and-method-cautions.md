# Sample size and method cautions

Small pilots can inform decisions without pretending to be controlled studies. State plainly what the sample can and cannot establish.

## Do not

- Do not invent statistical significance.
- Do not recommend p-values automatically.
- Do not claim representativeness from convenience samples.
- Do not treat repeated outputs from one case as independent users.
- Do not treat satisfaction as proof of accuracy or learning.
- Do not average critical and trivial errors together.
- Do not use percentages without denominators.
- Do not draw broad conclusions from small pilots.
- Do not change the sample silently after seeing results.

## Instead

- Report counts with denominators ("3 of 20 cases," not "15%").
- Describe how cases were selected and what population they represent.
- Distinguish "no critical failures observed in 20 cases" from "safe."
- Treat a small pilot as a signal that supports a bounded decision (continue, revise, stop, or escalate), not proof.
- Recommend methodological or statistical consultation when the decision requires formal inference.

## What a small pilot can establish

- The presence of at least one critical failure (very informative).
- Gross unsuitability (if it fails common cases, larger samples will not rescue it).
- A rough signal of direction and burden to decide whether a larger evaluation is warranted.

## What it cannot establish

- Precise error rates.
- Rare-event frequencies.
- Generalization across users, tasks, or time.
- Institutional readiness.

State these limits in every results report and decision memo.
