# Teaching and learning pilots

Pilots of AI in teaching add a duty to students: the pilot must not disadvantage them, expose their data, or substitute engagement metrics for learning evidence.

## Define the learning decision

State what teaching decision the pilot supports, for example: whether to offer an AI formative-feedback option in one seminar next term; whether an AI practice tool helps students prepare for an exercise.

## Learning evidence, not satisfaction

- Distinguish student satisfaction from actual learning.
- Where possible, use a learning measure (performance on a defined task) rather than "students liked it."
- Beware novelty effects; a new tool often draws enthusiasm that fades.

## Fairness and consent

- Provide a genuine non-AI alternative so no student is forced into an AI workflow.
- Do not make the pilot affect grades unless that is explicitly designed, disclosed, and approved.
- Protect student data; prefer no student personal data, and never use student records without authorized approval.
- Be transparent with students about what is being piloted and why.

## Accuracy still matters

If the tool gives students substantive content (explanations, feedback on legal reasoning), test it for fabricated authority, omissions, and misstatements of current law, exactly as any other pilot.
