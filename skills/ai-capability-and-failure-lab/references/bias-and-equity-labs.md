# Bias and equity labs

## Purpose

Test whether materially equivalent examples receive uneven treatment—without inflammatory content or overgeneralization from anecdotes.

## Required design elements

1. **Clear hypothesis** — what difference, if any, do you predict?
2. **Controlled variations** — change only the relevant attribute across examples
3. **Multiple examples** — more than one paired comparison when possible
4. **Confounding review** — note other differences that could explain results
5. **Caution about generalization** — one result does not establish group-level claims

## What not to do

- Do not claim bias against protected groups from a single anecdote
- Do not use unnecessary inflammatory scenarios
- Do not treat uneven performance as proof of intent

## Example safe patterns (synthetic)

- Equivalent contract clauses with different party names or regions (fictional)
- Same legal issue, different industries in hypothetical facts
- Parallel student writing samples with controlled quality (fictional, de-identified composites)

## Equity and access in teaching labs

Note whether all students can access the same tool tier or mode. Tool tier may affect results.

## Stanford Lens

Could the experiment reinforce false conclusions about groups? Is the activity accessible?

## Profile language

Use cautious findings: uneven treatment observed in this test set; additional representative testing warranted; result insufficient to support a broader conclusion.
