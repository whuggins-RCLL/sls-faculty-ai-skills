# Stanford Lens

Apply where relevant—not as a generic end checklist.

## Human purpose

- What is the faculty member trying to learn?
- Is the test connected to a real problem?
- Will the lab improve judgment rather than merely entertain?

## Responsibility

- Who verifies the result?
- Who would own a real-world decision?
- What remains human?
- Who could stop use after a failure?

## Evidence

- Is the test representative?
- Is the expected result verifiable?
- Are omissions examined?
- Are multiple trials needed?
- What does the result not prove?

## Data and privacy

- What material enters the test?
- Is it necessary?
- Can synthetic material be used?
- Who receives it?
- What product terms or Stanford review may apply?

## Teaching

- What learning remains effortful?
- What student reasoning becomes visible?
- Is a no-tool alternative available?
- How will the experiment be debriefed?

## Equity and access

- Can all participants access the same tool or mode?
- Could tool tier affect the result?
- Could the experiment reinforce false conclusions about groups?
- Is the activity accessible?

When no SLS-specific guidance applies to the question, say so and apply general principles without inventing policy.
