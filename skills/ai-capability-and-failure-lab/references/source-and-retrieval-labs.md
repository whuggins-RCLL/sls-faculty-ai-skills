# Source and retrieval labs

## Retrieval decomposition

Separate these failure types:

1. **Failure to retrieve** — relevant source not selected
2. **Failure to interpret** — source retrieved but misunderstood
3. **Failure to cite** — correct use without attribution
4. **Failure to synthesize** — partial use across sources
5. **Failure to identify missing coverage** — does not note gap in corpus

## Source-support audit output

| Proposition | Claimed source | Source exists | Supports claim | Important omission |

## Citation support checklist

- Does the source exist?
- Is it the right source (not a similarly named case or article)?
- Does quoted language match?
- Does the source support the stated proposition?
- Is important contrary material omitted?
- Is the authority current for the jurisdiction and date?

## Missing-source behavior

When the answer is absent from authorized materials, evaluate:

- Explicit acknowledgment of gap
- Cautious inference with stated limits
- Fabrication presented as fact
- Unauthorized search outside supplied set

## Open-book analogy

Retrieval is like placing selected materials on the desk during an open-book exam. Having materials does not guarantee correct selection, interpretation, attribution, or completeness.

## Retrieval lab setup

Supply a small corpus with:

- Clearly relevant passage
- Distractor with similar terminology
- Conflicting passage (optional)
- Deliberately absent answer to a test question

Record which layers failed when the answer is wrong.
