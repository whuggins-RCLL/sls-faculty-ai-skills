# Empirical claim review

When examining a study or empirical claim, review:

- Research question
- Study population and sample size
- Sampling method
- Task and materials
- Baseline and comparator
- Outcome and error definitions
- Study duration
- Statistical uncertainty when reported
- Missing data
- Researcher or sponsor interest
- Human-review role
- Model or product version
- Prompting or setup
- Replicability
- External validity
- Whether headline overstates the paper

## Plain-language translation

Example: The study shows selected participants completed this particular task faster under these conditions. It does not by itself establish that the product will reduce total faculty workload after verification, correction, training, and implementation.

## Time-savings claims

Ask: which task; was review time included; correction time; setup and training; failed attempts; baseline; experience levels; output quality held constant; work shifted elsewhere; additional monitoring or governance work.

## Accuracy claims

Ask: what counts as correct; unit measured; omissions counted; partial answers; fabricated citations; error severity; base rate; test set representativeness; training exposure to benchmark; model version; reproducibility.

## Abstract-only limits

State limits of evaluating without full paper. Do not infer unreported methods or results.

## Graph without context

Request source, axes, population, method, and date. Do not infer conclusion from visual appearance alone.
