# Legal and policy claims

## Legal claims

When a claim depends on current law:

- Use live web research
- Prefer primary authority
- Identify jurisdiction and date
- Distinguish holding from commentary
- Distinguish binding from persuasive authority
- Identify unsettled questions
- Avoid categorical legal conclusion from headline
- State when deeper legal research is required

The Skill may examine whether evidence supports a legal claim. It must not replace full legal research or provide unsupported definitive legal opinion.

Hand off when the user's primary task is substantive legal research without a specific claim to test.

## Policy and institutional claims

For Stanford or enterprise policy claims:

- Search current official sources
- Distinguish SLS guidance, university policy, service information, vendor claim, user report
- Identify product tier, data class, intended use
- Do not infer prohibition from absence of public listing

Use: I did not locate a current public Stanford source establishing that claim. That does not prove the claim is false, but it means the claim should not be relied on without confirmation for the specific account, data type, and intended use.

## Normative claims

When primarily normative, do not pretend evidence alone resolves it.

Identify: value protected; competing value; rights or duties; distribution of benefits and harms; institutional role; professional obligation; feasibility; alternatives; evidence relevant to value judgment; reasonable disagreement.

Findings: Primarily normative; Normative position supported by stated values; Empirical premise requires verification; Legal premise requires verification; Tradeoff not yet addressed.
