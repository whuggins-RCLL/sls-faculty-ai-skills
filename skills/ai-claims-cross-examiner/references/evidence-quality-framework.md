# Evidence quality framework

Evaluate evidence across: relevance; source authority; independence; transparency; method quality; sample appropriateness; task representativeness; comparator quality; measurement validity; reproducibility; currency; generalizability; conflict of interest; missing failure information; applicability to the faculty decision.

## Evidence ladder

| Level | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 Assertion | Unverified statement | Marketing copy, anecdote, social post |
| 2 Demonstration | Shows possibility on selected examples | Demo, cherry-picked case |
| 3 Internal evaluation | Vendor or sponsor testing | May be useful; review methods and COI |
| 4 Independent evaluation | Transparent external testing | Published benchmark with methods |
| 5 Replicated or multi-source | Consistent across contexts | Multiple independent studies |
| 6 Representative deployment | Realistic workflow evidence | Includes review burden and failure costs |

Do not treat the ladder as an automatic numerical score. A well-designed internal evaluation may be more relevant than an unrelated published benchmark.

## Capability, reliability, deployment

- **Capability evidence:** system can produce a result under tested conditions.
- **Reliability evidence:** how consistently it performs across representative situations.
- **Deployment evidence:** complete workflow including humans, data, permissions, review, cost, failure handling.

Do not infer deployment from capability alone.

## Core distinctions

- Not established ≠ false
- Possible ≠ demonstrated
- Works sometimes ≠ works reliably
- Capability ≠ appropriate use
- Model capability ≠ complete system outcome
- Technical feasibility ≠ institutional value
- Prediction ≠ present fact
- Normative judgment ≠ empirical evidence
- Absence of public evidence ≠ proof of absence

## Steelman and limits

Before criticizing, state the strongest defensible version. Also list what the claim does not establish.
