# Source and conflict analysis

## Source classification

Label each source: official Stanford; primary legal authority; original research; official technical documentation; government or standards; independent evaluation; systematic review; vendor documentation; credible independent reporting; user-provided; stable background; unknown.

Also classify: confirmed by current documentation; vendor-claimed; independently evaluated; reasonable inference; not publicly confirmed; requires institutional confirmation.

## When sources disagree

1. State the disagreement explicitly.
2. Identify differences in model version, product tier, prompt or task, dataset, participants, success definition, date, method, funding.
3. Explain whether findings may both be valid in different contexts.
4. Avoid selecting one result solely because it supports a preferred outcome.
5. State what evidence would resolve the disagreement.

## Independence check

Vendor claims about their own product are not independent evidence. Sponsored benchmarks require method and conflict-of-interest review. Search for outside evaluation when evaluating performance claims.

## Headline versus source

Compare headline to underlying study, report, or announcement. Note overstatement, missing qualifiers, and omitted limitations.

## Social media and screenshots

Treat as assertion until original evidence is located. Popularity is not support. Request or search for primary source, date, and context.
