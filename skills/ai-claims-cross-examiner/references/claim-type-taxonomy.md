# Claim type taxonomy

Classify using one or more categories. Each type implies different evidence standards.

## Definitional claim

Examples: An agent is an autonomous AI employee; RAG is a form of model training.

Question: Is the term used accurately and consistently?

## Technical capability claim

Examples: This model can analyze legal documents; The system can reason over long contexts.

Question: What capability, on which task, under which conditions?

## Reliability claim

Examples: The system is accurate; Retrieval prevents hallucinations.

Question: How often, compared with what standard, and what errors remain?

## Comparative claim

Examples: AI is better than lawyers; Product A is more accurate than Product B.

Question: Better at what, measured how, using which participants and versions?

## Causal claim

Examples: AI improves learning; AI reduces access-to-justice gaps; AI causes deskilling.

Question: Does evidence show causation, association, mechanism, or speculation?

## Empirical claim

Examples: Faculty save 40 percent of their time; Students who use AI perform worse.

Question: What study, sample, task, baseline, and measurement support this?

## Benchmark claim

Examples: The model scored 90 percent on a legal benchmark; The model passed the bar exam.

Question: What does the benchmark test, and how well does it represent real work?

## Legal claim

Examples: Using this system violates copyright law; AI-generated work cannot receive copyright protection.

Question: Which jurisdiction, authority, factual context, and date?

## Policy or institutional claim

Examples: Stanford does not permit this tool; Enterprise accounts are approved for confidential information.

Question: Which authoritative current policy, service tier, data class, and use?

## Normative claim

Examples: Faculty should never use AI to give feedback; Law schools have a duty to teach generative AI.

Question: Which values, obligations, tradeoffs, and assumptions support the position?

## Organizational claim

Examples: Building our own system would give us control; AI will reduce staffing needs.

Question: Which workflow, costs, dependencies, and governance arrangements?

## Economic claim

Examples: AI will save money; Hosting an open model is cheaper.

Question: Compared with what total cost, including review, maintenance, and failure?

## Marketing claim

Examples: The product is proprietary; The system is enterprise-grade and hallucination-free.

Question: How are terms defined, and what evidence supports them?

## Predictive claim

Examples: AI will replace junior lawyers in five years; Agents will transform legal education.

Question: What assumptions drive the forecast, and what could change it?

## Universal or absolute claim

Examples: AI always hallucinates; AI cannot reason; Larger models are always better.

Question: Is one counterexample enough to disprove the wording, and what narrower claim might be defensible?

## Multi-type claims

Many real claims combine types. Identify each component and evaluate separately. Example: “Enterprise AI is safe for student records” combines marketing, policy, reliability, and institutional claims.
