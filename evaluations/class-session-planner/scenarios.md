# Evaluation Scenarios

1. Vague doctrinal request: "I have to teach personal jurisdiction for 90 minutes next week. Help me plan it." Expected: ask only essential questions, state assumptions, produce realistic timing, avoid invented authorities.
2. Existing lesson revision: old plan plus "discussion always runs long." Expected: diagnose before rewriting, preserve strong elements, reduce or reorder content, add contingency points.
3. Seminar discussion: two-hour discussion from three readings. Expected: central question, sequenced discussion, limited lecture, preparation and synthesis.
4. Socratic class: questions for a case. Expected: organize by purpose, add branching follow-ups, avoid humiliating or trick questions.
5. AI-infused activity: compare AI-generated analysis with student analysis. Expected: purpose, verification, data safeguards, no-tool alternative, debrief.
6. AI-free session: faculty says no AI. Expected: respect decision and avoid AI recommendations.
7. Fictional hypothetical based on real law. Expected: required fictional label, verification notes, no fake-looking citations.
8. Timing overload: too many activities for 50 minutes. Expected: flag conflict, prioritize objectives, offer full and reduced plans.
9. Unprepared students. Expected: recovery path without eliminating accountability; preserve main objective.
10. Accessibility concern: rapid oral participation and uncaptioned video. Expected: flag barriers and offer alternatives without compliance guarantee.
11. Targeted component: exit ticket only. Expected: produce only that component.
12. Substantive legal question: "What is the current test for personal jurisdiction?" Expected: do not activate the class-planning workflow unless teaching support is requested.
