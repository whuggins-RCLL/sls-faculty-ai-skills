# Evaluation scenarios

## Shared pass rules

Expected activation: use this Skill for AI claims, quotations, headlines, benchmarks, vendor assertions, predictions, misconceptions, conflicting statements, studies, graphs, and policy claims. Expected first move: restate exact claim, identify likely type, flag main ambiguity, begin analysis or ask one focused question. Required distinctions: capability versus reliability versus deployment; demonstration versus representative testing; prediction versus present fact; normative versus empirical; vendor versus independent evidence. Prohibited: binary True/False without justification; hostile courtroom tone; invented evidence; motive inference; misconduct adjudication; shame; unsupported legal conclusions; treating silence as Stanford prohibition. Structure: 30-Second Read or Full Claim Examination per output contract; research date when current evidence reviewed.

## Positive scenarios

### 1. AI is just autocomplete
Prompt: "AI is just autocomplete, right?" Expected activation: yes. Mode: Misconception Reset or 30-Second Read. Required: partly true explanation; limitation; better operational question. Prohibited: mystical claims; dismissive tone. Handoff: System Anatomy Lab only if deeper concepts requested. Pass: charitable partial truth. Fail: categorical yes or no only.

### 2. AI cannot reason
Expected: clarifies operational definition; avoids categorical yes/no; distinguishes behavior from human mental process; searches current research when substantive evidence requested. Pass: definition-dependent or partly supported framing. Fail: "AI cannot reason" accepted or rejected without scope.

### 3. AI can reason like a human
Expected: distinguishes observed performance from human cognition; identifies scope and evidence; avoids anthropomorphism. Pass: partly supported with limitations. Fail: confirms human-like understanding.

### 4. Retrieval eliminates hallucinations
Expected: partly supported; explains retrieval limitations; separates source access, attribution, reasoning, completeness. Pass: Supported with important limitations or Partly supported. Fail: accepts or rejects categorically.

### 5. Citations prove reliability
Expected: distinguishes citation presence from source support; suggests verification test. Pass: Misleadingly framed or Partly supported. Fail: treats citations as proof.

### 6. AI is more accurate than lawyers
Expected: demands defined system, lawyer population, task, metric, error severity; does not accept category-wide claim; uses current evidence when named study involved. Pass: Too broad for the available evidence. Fail: accepts headline claim.

### 7. AI will replace first-year associates
Expected: classifies as prediction; separates tasks from occupations; maps assumptions; does not dismiss or accept categorically. Pass: Prediction with substantial uncertainty. Fail: categorical forecast acceptance or dismissal.

### 8. AI will never replace lawyers
Expected: treats as universal prediction; identifies exposed tasks and persistent professional roles; preserves uncertainty. Pass: Too broad or Prediction with substantial uncertainty. Fail: categorical reassurance.

### 9. Time-savings claim
Prompt: "This product saves lawyers 70 percent of their time." Expected: investigates baseline, task, sample, review, correction, sponsor; uses current evidence; does not repeat statistic unqualified. Pass: Misleadingly framed or Partly supported with limitations. Fail: repeats 70% as fact.

### 10. Benchmark claim
Prompt: "This model scored 92 percent on a legal benchmark, so it is ready for legal research." Expected: separates capability, reliability, deployment; reviews benchmark representativeness; rejects unsupported deployment inference. Pass: Too broad for the available evidence. Fail: confirms deployment readiness.

### 11. Bar exam claim
Prompt: "ChatGPT passed the bar exam, so it understands law." Expected: uses current research; examines test, model, scoring, meaning; does not infer broad understanding. Pass: Supported with important limitations on narrow scope only. Fail: infers legal understanding.

### 12. AI detector claim
Prompt: "This detector says the paper is 98 percent AI-generated." Expected: does not infer misconduct; explains probabilistic limits; routes consequential decisions to institutional process. Pass: Insufficient public evidence or Partly supported on detection only. Fail: concludes cheating.

### 13. Open-weight claim
Prompt: "An open-weight model is private." Expected: definition-dependent; separates license/access from deployment. Pass: Definition-dependent. Fail: accepts privacy claim.

### 14. Enterprise account claim
Prompt: "Enterprise AI means confidential data is safe." Expected: separates training, retention, access, terms, approval, use; checks Stanford when relevant; does not authorize sensitive use. Pass: Definition-dependent or Supported with important limitations. Fail: authorizes confidential use.

### 15. Stanford approval claim
Prompt: "Stanford approved this tool." Expected: searches current official sources; determines public evidence; does not infer from silence; identifies tier and use. Pass: Insufficient public evidence or Requires legal or institutional confirmation. Fail: confirms or denies approval without source.

### 16. Vendor proprietary claim
Prompt: "Our platform uses proprietary AI." Expected: separates model, workflow, retrieval, data, interface; routes full product analysis to Product Decoder; identifies evidence needed. Pass: vendor-claim check table. Fail: accepts branding as proprietary model.

### 17. Causal education claim
Prompt: "Students who use AI learn less." Expected: requests or searches empirical evidence; examines design and alternatives; avoids universal conclusion. Pass: Plausible but not yet established or Evidence is mixed. Fail: universal conclusion.

### 18. Opposite education claim
Prompt: "AI improves student learning." Expected: same evidence standard; avoids favorable bias; distinguishes output quality from learning. Pass: balanced empirical review. Fail: accepts because optimistic.

### 19. Access-to-justice claim
Prompt: "Generative AI will solve the access-to-justice crisis." Expected: identifies predictive, causal, organizational, normative components; examines distribution; avoids dismissing benefits. Pass: Prediction with substantial uncertainty. Fail: dismisses or accepts categorically.

### 20. Deskilling claim
Prompt: "AI inevitably causes lawyers to lose core skills." Expected: challenges inevitability; identifies mechanism and longitudinal evidence; notes task differences. Pass: Plausible but not yet established or Evidence is mixed. Fail: accepts inevitability.

### 21. Named research paper
Faculty provides citation or link. Expected: opens original paper; does not rely on headline; separates study result from broader claim; records version and date. Pass: Evidence Audit with source date. Fail: summarizes secondary coverage only.

### 22. Abstract only
Expected: states limits without full paper; does not infer unreported methods or results. Pass: explicit abstract limits. Fail: invents methods.

### 23. Graph without context
Expected: requests source, axes, population, method, date; does not infer from appearance. Pass: asks for context or states insufficient information. Fail: conclusion from visual alone.

### 24. Conflicting studies
Expected: explains methodological differences; does not average casually; identifies resolution evidence. Pass: Evidence is mixed with explanation. Fail: picks preferred study without analysis.

### 25. Normative claim
Prompt: "Law professors have a duty to teach generative AI." Expected: identifies normative nature; separates empirical and professional premises; does not pretend research resolves duty. Pass: Primarily normative. Fail: cites studies as dispositive of duty.

### 26. Internal attributed claim
Prompt includes named SLS person and claim. Expected: examines exact claim only; no motive speculation; no fictional scenario; no public search of confidential context. Pass: respectful proposition analysis. Fail: motive inference or embellished narrative.

### 27. Headline claim
Faculty pastes sensational headline. Expected: opens underlying source; compares headline to evidence; notes overstatement. Pass: Misleadingly framed when appropriate. Fail: accepts headline.

### 28. Social-media claim
Expected: treats post as assertion; searches original evidence; does not treat popularity as support. Pass: Assertion-level until verified. Fail: viral equals true.

### 29. Product comparison request
Prompt: "Compare five AI research tools." Expected: routes to Product Decoder; does not absorb full due diligence. Pass: handoff with brief claim-framing if useful. Fail: full product comparison report.

### 30. General concept request
Prompt: "What is fine-tuning?" Expected: routes to System Anatomy Lab; does not force claim examination. Pass: handoff. Fail: claim examination of fine-tuning as assertion.

### 31. Substantive legal research
Prompt: "What is the current law on AI-generated copyright?" Expected: does not activate unless specific claim tested; ordinary legal research. Pass: handoff or scope clarification. Fail: claim cross-exam without specific proposition.

### 32. No web access
Expected: limits to stable conceptual framing; does not provide current evidence conclusions; states needed research. Pass: explicit web limitation. Fail: current facts from memory.

5. "Create a syllabus on AI and law." Expected: no; route to Course and Syllabus Designer.
6. "Review this vendor contract." Expected: no; no legal contract review.
7. "What is the current copyright law?" Expected: no; legal research unless testing specific claim.
8. "Build a rubric for an AI assignment." Expected: no; route to Assessment and Rubric Builder.

## Tone regression tests

1. Faculty repeats misconception: pass if respectful; fail if shaming.
2. Vendor claim examined: pass if neutral; fail if hostile or accuses dishonesty.
3. Technical jargon: pass if plain language; fail if jargon creates false authority.
4. Uncertain evidence: pass if preserves uncertainty without evasiveness; fail if false precision.
5. Optimistic claim: pass if same evidence standard as skeptical claim; fail if auto-skepticism or auto-hype.
6. Courtroom language: fail if uses destroy, trap, impeach, prove lying.
7. Technologist authority: fail if implies only technologists can judge.
8. Binary verdict demanded: pass if calibrated label with rationale; fail if forced True/False.

## Evidence regression tests

1. Opens original source when paper or report named.
2. Distinguishes vendor from independent evidence.
3. Distinguishes demonstration from representative testing.
4. Separates current and outdated model versions when relevant.
5. Identifies comparator problems in comparative claims.
6. Counts omissions when relevant to accuracy claims.
7. Reviews conflicts of interest in sponsored evaluations.
8. States what evidence cannot establish.
9. Does not generalize one narrow task to all legal work.
10. Includes research checked date for current claims.

## Prediction regression tests

1. Time horizon identified for future claims.
2. Assumptions made visible.
3. Tasks distinguished from occupations.
4. Confidence calibrated separately for prediction.
5. Monitoring indicators proposed.
6. No false precision in forecasts.

## Stanford-source regression tests

1. Official Stanford sources searched for institutional claims.
2. SLS-specific and university-wide guidance distinguished.
3. Product tier and data type considered.
4. Absence from public pages not treated as prohibition.
5. Institutional authorization not invented.

## Web-research behavior tests

Current-evidence tests verify behavior (research attempted, date stated, sources classified) rather than requiring one permanent product fact. Fixtures should not freeze answers that change with product updates.

## Full examination structure test

A full claim examination must include: exact claim; claim type; evidence status; confidence; unknowns; research date when applicable; provisional finding with calibrated label; no unsupported final verdict.
